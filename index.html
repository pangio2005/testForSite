Executive Summary

Reimagining how LEO data connects the world üåç

Satellites orbit Earth every few minutes, collecting massive amounts of environmental and spatial data. Yet only a small portion ever reaches scientists, governments, or companies who could use it. Proprietary systems and fragmented ground coverage prevent efficient use, creating barriers for innovation and global monitoring.

OpenOrbit is a new model that invites collaboration instead of competition. Partners contribute satellites, funding, or ground nodes to join the shared system. In return, they gain access to a growing pool of high-quality data. Each addition strengthens the network, improving coverage, reliability, and value for everyone involved.

Network Flow

How data and collaboration move through OpenOrbit üîÅ

OpenOrbit acts as a central link that brings together all levels of participants. Large companies contribute funding and satellite resources, connecting directly with OpenOrbit and the shared Data Interface. This interface manages transactions, data validation, and fair access.

Satellites and distributed data centers collect information and feed it through the network. Smaller organizations, research teams, and start-ups use the same interface to access data in real time. This ensures that both large and small players benefit equally from the same orbital infrastructure.

System Architecture

Three stages from orbit to insight üöÄ

Stage 1: Collection
Satellites operated by international partners coordinate observation schedules and share tasking. Participants can fund new missions or lease orbital time, providing continuous global coverage. Privacy is protected through built-in hardware limits and encryption standards.

Stage 2: Transmission
OpenOrbit uses a decentralized peer-to-peer ground network. Data collected by one ground node is replicated and shared through others in near real time. This expands uptime from only 10 percent of an orbit to about 60 percent, drastically improving speed and reliability.

Stage 3: Distribution
The OpenOrbit interface compiles and visualizes all received data. Large industries can integrate with APIs, while smaller companies and universities use intuitive dashboards. Differential access levels ensure fair use, sustainability, and financial accessibility.
